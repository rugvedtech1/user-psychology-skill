# F-Pattern and Z-Pattern Eye Movement - Complete Guide
# Purpose: Structure layouts based on how human eyes actually move

## THE SCIENCE BEHIND EYE PATTERNS

Jakob Nielsen's eyetracking studies (2006, updated 2017) using infrared
eye tracking cameras on thousands of users proved that people do not read
web pages — they scan them in predictable patterns. Designing against
these patterns means your most important content gets missed.

## F-PATTERN IN DEPTH

### What It Is
Users scan text-heavy pages in an F shape:
- First horizontal movement → reads first line nearly completely
- Second horizontal movement → shorter scan of a second line
- Vertical movement → scans down the left side of content

### Heat Map Interpretation
- Top-left corner → hottest zone, highest attention
- First line of any section → high attention
- Left edge of content → medium consistent attention
- Bottom-right → coldest zone, almost never seen

### When to Use F-Pattern
- Blog posts and articles
- Product listing pages (grid or list)
- Search results pages
- News feeds and dashboards
- Any page with multiple pieces of content to scan

### F-Pattern Implementation Rules

#### Headlines and Subheads
- Every subhead starts with the most important word
- Bad: "The amazing features of our product"
- Good: "Features that cut your workload by 50%"
- First word of every bullet point must carry meaning
- Bad: "• You will be able to track all your payments"
- Good: "• Track all payments in one dashboard"

#### Left-Side Priority
- Place navigation on left sidebar for desktop apps
- Key filters on left in product listing pages
- Most important metadata on left of list items
- Profile photos on left of testimonials and comments

#### Content Density
- Short paragraphs (3-4 lines maximum)
- One idea per paragraph
- Bullet points over long paragraphs
- Bold key phrases — users scan for bold text
- Never bury key information in the middle of long paragraphs

#### Line Length
- Optimal: 50-75 characters per line
- Too wide (100+ chars) → eyes lose track, scanning breaks down
- Too narrow (30 chars) → too many line breaks, rhythm disrupted

## Z-PATTERN IN DEPTH

### What It Is
On simple, low-text pages users follow a Z shape:
- Start at top-left → move to top-right (first horizontal)
- Diagonal movement down-left (the diagonal)
- Move from bottom-left → to bottom-right (second horizontal)

### When to Use Z-Pattern
- Landing pages with single conversion goal
- Homepages
- Simple product pages
- Splash pages and coming soon pages
- Any page with one primary action

### Z-Pattern Layout Formula

#### Top Bar (First Horizontal of Z)
- Top-left: Logo (brand recognition anchor)
- Top-center: Navigation (if needed, minimal)
- Top-right: Primary CTA button ("Get Started", "Login")
- Background: Clean, no clutter

#### The Diagonal
- Large hero image or product screenshot
- Main value proposition headline
- Eyes travel naturally from top-right to bottom-left
- Place your strongest visual here

#### Bottom Bar (Second Horizontal of Z)
- Bottom-left: Supporting information, social proof snippet
- Bottom-right: PRIMARY CONVERSION CTA
- This is where the user's eye lands last before deciding
- Make bottom-right CTA unmissable

## LAYER PATTERN (Advanced)

### What It Is
Modern users on well-designed pages use a layer pattern:
- Scan headlines layer first (all H1, H2, H3)
- If interested, scan image captions layer
- If still interested, read body text
- Designed for users who are evaluating before committing

### Implementation
- Every section must have a scannable headline
- Headlines alone should tell the full story of the page
- Image captions should be descriptive and benefit-focused
- Body text is for users already convinced — do not rely on it for conversion

## MOBILE EYE PATTERNS

### Single Column Changes Everything
- Mobile forces F-pattern regardless of desktop layout
- Single column = users read more linearly top to bottom
- First screen (above fold) gets 80% of attention
- Below fold drops sharply — each scroll loses 20% of users

### Mobile-Specific Rules
- First 3 elements on mobile are critical: Logo + Headline + CTA
- Thumb zone: bottom third of screen is easiest to tap
- Place CTAs in thumb zone (bottom of screen)
- Avoid important content in top corners (hard to tap)
- Font minimum 16px — smaller forces zoom which breaks pattern

## COMBINING PATTERNS WITH CONTENT HIERARCHY

### Visual Hierarchy Supports Eye Patterns
1. Size → Largest element seen first
2. Color → High contrast draws eye before low contrast
3. Position → Top-left before bottom-right
4. Whitespace → Isolated elements get more attention
5. Movement → Animated elements draw eye (use carefully)

### Applying to Real Pages

#### Landing Page (Z-Pattern)
- Top-left: Logo
- Top-right: "Get Started Free" button
- Center: Hero headline + subheadline + hero image
- Bottom-left: "Trusted by 10,000+ teams" + logos
- Bottom-right: Large "Start Free Trial" CTA

#### Blog Post (F-Pattern)
- H1: Starts with most important keyword
- First paragraph: Contains entire summary of article
- Subheads: Every 200-300 words, start with key word
- Bullet points: For any list of 3+ items
- Bold: Key terms and actionable phrases
- CTA: After introduction, after conclusion, in sidebar

#### Product Listing Page (F-Pattern)
- Filters: Left sidebar
- Sort options: Top of grid
- Product cards: Price and rating visible without hover
- Most important products: Top-left position in grid

## EYE PATTERN AUDIT CHECKLIST

Before finalizing any page layout:
- [ ] Does the top-left contain brand/logo?
- [ ] Is the primary CTA in a Z-pattern endpoint (top-right or bottom-right)?
- [ ] Do all subheads start with meaningful words?
- [ ] Are paragraphs 3-4 lines maximum?
- [ ] Is line length between 50-75 characters?
- [ ] Does mobile show the 3 critical elements first?
- [ ] Can a user understand the page by reading only headlines?
- [ ] Is the most important content in the left half of the layout?
